Ginger Edges out Sugar in List of 5 Most Popular Ingredients

By Adrian Sas
Dec. 11, 2014

Over 16,000 recipes have appeared in The New York Times over the last three decades. They are now conveniently available for consultation, or inspiration on their new microsite, NYT Cooking (cooking.nytimes.com) which launched in the summer of 2014. An analysis of data from the archive upon which the site was built, revealed that in the last 3 years ginger has taken the place of sugar as one of the top five most frequently listed ingredients in published recipes.

Onion remains ubiquity's champion, although it fell by one percentage point in recent years. The steady constants, salt followed by garlic, round out the top three most abundantly relied upon ingredients since 1981, when the digital archive begins. Before 2010, sugar was at number 4, called upon in 13% of all recipes. After 2010, the sweet stuff drops from that sweet spot to sixth place appearing in only 8% of recipes. Olive oil rises a notch to 4th place after 2010 when ginger supplants sugar and takes 5th place.

Ginger is the rootstalk of a rhizomatous plant. Like its cousin turmeric, it is often dried and pulverized and sold commercially as powder in spice jars. Unlike turmeric, "ground" or "powdered ginger," as the New York Times sometimes distinguishes the spice's name when prompting its use in this form, commonly appears in its recipes for baked goods, think gingersnaps or gingerbread. "Candied" or "crystallized" pieces of the tuber are also designated for desserts and fruit compotes. The other half of the time, The Times instructs that the root should occasionally be pickled, or more popularly, used fresh, sliced, diced or minced and added to savory dishes.

Dr. Krishnendu Ray, Chair of the Department of Nutrition, Food Studies & Public Health at New York University explained that baking with ginger indicates a method "derived from Northern Europe, while the use of fresh ginger is associated with traditionally Asian technique."

Here in New York, where the cliched term melting pot is ever so apt when it comes to our culinary tradition, NYT Cooking recently featured the Ethiopian born, Swedish reared, New York Chef Marcus Samuelsson's recipe for a Bolivian staple. "One tablespoon of minced peeled ginger" delivers much of the flavor: Marcus Samuelssom's Quinoa with Broccoli, Cauliflower and Toasted Coconut.
